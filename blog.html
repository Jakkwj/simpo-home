Plotting Functionality Optimized for Enhanced Stability‚Äã

In SIMPO, after each computation, the program automatically generates a series of complex graphics based on the calculation engine. To maximize plotting efficiency, we've introduced multiprocessing (leveraging multi-core CPUs for parallel processing) to accelerate this process.

However, in certain specific scenarios, multiprocessing plotting can trigger a BrokenProcessPool error, leading to plotting interruptions. To address this critical issue, we've optimized the plotting functionality in SIMPO 1.0.1. This version ensures that when the program encounters this error, it will automatically switch to the main process, guaranteeing the completion of the plotting procedure.

System Platform Differences and Recommendations‚Äã

We've observed that BrokenProcessPool errors are more common on Windows (and rare on Ubuntu). This is likely due to the difference in how Windows uses spawn to start child processes, whereas Linux typically uses fork.

Consequently, users on Ubuntu systems generally benefit from faster computation speeds, more stable multiprocessing operations, and better compatibility.

For a smoother, faster, and more stable computing experience, we strongly recommend performing SIMPO computations within an Ubuntu environment.

First Paper on SIMPO Published

November 27, 2025 ¬∑ 14 min read

Professor Feng Jiang's team from the School of Environmental Science and Engineering at Sun Yat-sen University has published a cover article titled "SIMPO‚ÄîA Simplified Intelligent Modelling Platform Online for Code-free and Shareable Wastewater Treatment Process Modelling" in Volume 1, Issue 4 of Water & Ecology.

Water & Ecology Volume 1, Issue 4 Cover

The article introduces SIMPO, a code-free intelligent modeling and sharing platform developed by Professor Feng Jiang's team for the mathematical simulation of wastewater treatment processes. SIMPO is a free, open-source, and shareable SaaS (Software as a Service) cloud platform. It aims to provide everyone with zero-threshold access to a professional-grade wastewater treatment process modeling system. This empowers faculty and students, scientists, and engineers in the field of environmental engineering to more easily engage in learning, research, and improvement of wastewater treatment processes, thereby lowering the barriers to the global dissemination of technology. Following its release, the article has been widely reposted by international science media outlets such as EurekAlert! .

https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1106824

DOI: 10.1016/j.wateco.2025.100021

First Authors: Jun Wang, Hao Li

Corresponding Author: Feng Jiang

Affiliation: School of Environmental Science & Engineering, Sun Yat-sen University

Graphical Abstract‚Äã

Highlights

  1. SIMPO is an open, cloud-based, code-free, and shareable platform for wastewater treatment modelling.
  2. SIMPO features a drag-and-drop graphical user interfaces (GUI), adaptive solvers, and automated error-proofing.
  3. Advanced algorithms support sensitivity analysis, uncertainty assessment and parameter estimation.
  4. The platform can be widely applied in scientific research, education, engineering optimization, and peer review.

Article Summary‚Äã

Wastewater treatment process models, such as the Activated Sludge Model (ASM) series, are crucial tools for research, simulation, and design in environmental engineering. However, as these models become increasingly complex, their development, application, and validation pose significant challenges for researchers, engineers, and even peer reviewers. Traditional software often suffers from steep learning curves, cumbersome operations, a lack of collaborative features, and high costs. This not only hinders the widespread adoption and innovation of these models but also restricts the application and improvement of water treatment technologies in developing countries. Addressing this critical need, the author's team has developed SIMPO‚Äî a free, zero-barrier, open, and shareable SaaS cloud platform. The platform aims to significantly lower the threshold for wastewater treatment process modeling through a code-free, drag-and-drop graphical interface, while integrating advanced algorithms to ensure the professionalism, accuracy, and reliability of the models.

Visual Guide‚Äã

SIMPO Makes Water Treatment Process Modeling as Simple as "Building Blocks"‚Äã

Compared to traditional modeling software like AQUASIM, SIMPO offers a simpler, more convenient, and smoother modeling process. SIMPO simplifies complex modeling into intuitive drag-and-drop operations, combined with an AI-driven core for model building, achieving "zero-barrier" modeling:

  • AQUASIM: Users must manually input variables, define processes, partition units, set connections, configure time steps, and edit plots within multi-level dialog boxes, resulting in a complex and non-intuitive workflow.
  • SIMPO: Users only need to follow three straightforward steps: "Edit Model (BioModel)" ‚Üí "Drag-and-Drop to Build Process (DataSet)" ‚Üí "Automatically Generate Project and Calculate (Project)". The platform automatically handles time stepping and result plotting.
Figure 1. Comparison of modelling processes between AQUASIM and SIMPO.

SIMPO's platform is powerful, covering the entire process from input, simulation, to evaluation and sharing. Figures 2 and 3 illustrate SIMPO's core operational interfaces:

  • Visual Matrix Editing: Chemical stoichiometric and rate expressions are edited in a matrix format, consistent with ASM series models. The system provides color block highlighting to effectively prevent input errors.
  • Drag-and-Drop Flow Chart Design:: Users can rapidly build complex wastewater treatment process flowsheet designs by simply dragging and dropping with the mouse, much like assembling building blocks.
Figure 2. The model editing interface of SIMPO.
Figure 3. The drag-and-drop flowchart editing interface of SIMPO: (A) module addition menu; (B) add process components; (C) connection process flow; (D) fill in the flows in the connections.

To further lower the learning curve for water treatment process modeling, the team has recently developed an AI-assisted modeling function. SIMPO, by leveraging large language models such as Gemini 3, can automatically read text and diagrams from research papers to generate biochemical models (Biomodel).

Accuracy and Performance Guaranteed‚Äã

The reliability of a new tool must be rigorously validated. The research team selected a classic case study to compare SIMPO's simulation results against the industry benchmark software, AQUASIM.

As shown in Figure 4, in batch and continuous reactor simulations of the biological nutrient removal model, SIMPO's simulation curves almost perfectly overlap with those of AQUASIM, achieving a weighted Nash-Sutcliffe efficiency (WNSE) above 0.999. This demonstrates that SIMPO maintains operational simplicity while matching the computational accuracy of professional software.

"One-Click Access" to Advanced Analysis Features‚Äã

For advanced analytical needs, SIMPO integrates professional algorithms such as sensitivity analysis, uncertainty assessment, and parameter estimation.

Sensitivity Analysis

In SIMPO, sensitivity analysis is used to identify sensitive and highly correlated parameters, thereby reducing the number of parameters to be evaluated in subsequent steps and improving computational efficiency.

  • Through OAT (One at a Time) single-factor analysis, key parameters with the greatest impact on model output can be quickly identified.
  • Correlation heatmaps help discover highly correlated parameter groups, thus reducing the number of parameters to be estimated and enhancing the efficiency of subsequent analyses.
Figure 7. (A) The outcomes of the OAT sensitivity analysis in SIMPO; (B) The weighted sum of the integration of OAT curves in SIMPO; (C) The pairwise correlation heat map in SIMPO; (D) The outcomes of the OAT sensitivity analysis in AQUASIM.

Uncertainty Assessment

SIMPO provides professional uncertainty assessment algorithms. Through Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) and Monte Carlo (MC) simulations, it can be used to evaluate how parameter uncertainty propagates to model outputs, analyze parameter and model output distributions, identify equifinality, and narrow down parameter ranges. The results of uncertainty assessment are intuitively evaluated from different angles through various visual forms, including Probability Density Function (PDF) plots, Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) plots, Band plots, Density plots, and Skeleton plots.

Figure 8. (A) The PDF and CDF plots of the uncertainty assessment in SIMPO; (B) The Band plots; (C) The Density plots; (D) The Skeleton plots.

Parameter Estimation

SIMPO employs the Genetic Algorithm (GA) for parameter estimation. SIMPO offers various computational settings, allowing for a flexible balance between computational efficiency and solution accuracy. The computational results from several different evaluation methods are presented in Table 1:

  • The Large GA yields the highest accuracy but requires significant time due to its large scale, reaching a WNSE of 0.99161 in 267 seconds after approximately 1,394 iterations.
  • The Small GA has a smaller computational scale, resulting in slightly lower accuracy but extremely short computation times, achieving a WNSE of 0.99019 in just 0.83 seconds after only 25 iterations.
  • Uncertainty also achieves high accuracy but is the most time-consuming, reaching a WNSE of 0.99161 in 4,658 seconds after approximately 880,510 iterations.

The Introduction of SIMPO

June 28, 2025 ¬∑ 4 min read

Since 1987, the International Association on Water Quality (IAWQ) working group released a series of mathematical models for wastewater treatment processes (such as ASM1, ASM2, ASM2d, ASM3, and ADM1), which established a general framework for the removal of pollutants like organic matter, nitrogen, and phosphorus. These models have become powerful tools for the research and practice of wastewater treatment processes.

However, as research deepens, the scope of wastewater treatment process modeling has expanded to include various complex processes (such as nitrous oxide emissions, and sulfur autotrophic denitrification). Although these expansions have increased the applicability of the models, they have also introduced exponentially growing complexity. The biochemical stoichiometry matrix has become increasingly large, the number of model components and parameters has increased, and the computational demands have risen, creating significant obstacles for researchers and engineers.

Origin
  • SIMPO is initiated by Professor Jiang Feng's research team from the School of Environmental Science and Engineering at Sun Yat-sen University. It is an intelligent computing SaaS platform specifically designed for wastewater treatment process modelling.

To address these challenges and opportunities, we developed SIMPO (Simplified Intelligent Modelling Platform OnlineÔºåhttps://www.simpowater.org), a next-generation platform aimed at simplifying and promoting wastewater treatment modelling. SIMPO offers an intuitive drag-and-drop user interface, adaptive algorithms, and collaborative tools, bridging the gap between complex modeling capabilities and practical accessibility, enabling users to easily handle traditional ASM models and various modern cutting-edge models.

SIMPO's innovations address critical limitations of traditional platforms:

1„ÄÅInteraction and visualization of GUI provide a user-friendly modelling approach

  • Intuitive tools, such as candidate boxes and color coding, visually assist in correctly inputting variables and parameters in a complex matrix.
  • The drag-and-drop GUI simplifies the design of complex wastewater treatment processes to the point of simply adding and connecting tanks on a webpage.
  • The automatic drawing display helps users quickly, comprehensively, and intuitively understand the simulation and evaluation results.

2„ÄÅDynamic algorithm ensures the accuracy and efficiency of modelling

  • SIMPO adopts self-adjusting solvers to ensure computational efficiency without affecting accuracy, which is evaluated by weighted Nash Sutcliffe efficiency (WNSE).
  • Dynamic real time step size optimization can alleviate common pitfalls such as negative concentration, which is a frequent problem in traditional tools.

3„ÄÅAutomatic error-proofing and intuitive display help to quickly identify errors

  • Balance checking can help users review the rationality of stoichiometry matrices from a theoretical perspective.
  • NSE intuitively reflects the goodness-of-fit of various variables, and the automated and interactive display of results exposes the problem of certain variables being obscured by graphical scales.

4„ÄÅAdvanced evaluation algorithms bring comprehensive and systematic understanding of models

  • Sensitivity analysis identifies sensitive parameters and their correlations, reducing the number of parameters to enhance computational efficiency.
  • Uncertainty assessment evaluates the uncertainty of each parameter, determines the predicted range of model outputs, and refines parameter ranges for more accurate simulations.
  • Parameter estimation identifies the local optima of parameters that best fit the measured data, ensuring robust and reliable model calibration.

5„ÄÅCollaborative science facilitates the dissemination, exchange, and development of models

  • SIMPO supports version-controlled models, data and results sharing, provides public/private repositories, and advocates a new type of peer review workflow for modelling papers, in line with the principles of open science.

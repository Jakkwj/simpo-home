Modelling Methane Production and Sulfate Reduction in Anaerobic Granular Sludge Reactor with Ethanol as Electron Donor

DOI: 10.1038/srep35312

Authors: Jing Sun, Xiaohu Dai, Qilin Wang, Yuting Pan & Bing-Jie Ni

In this work, a mathematical model based on growth kinetics of microorganisms and substrates transportation through biofilms was developed to describe methane production and sulfate reduction with ethanol being a key electron donor. The model was calibrated and validated using experimental data from two case studies conducted in granule-based Upflow Anaerobic Sludge Blanket reactors. The results suggest that the developed model could satisfactorily describe methane and sulfide productions as well as ethanol and sulfate removals in both systems. The modeling results reveal a stratified distribution of methanogenic archaea, sulfate-reducing bacteria and fermentative bacteria in the anaerobic granular sludge and the relative abundances of these microorganisms vary with substrate concentrations. It also indicates sulfate-reducing bacteria can successfully outcompete fermentative bacteria for ethanol utilization when COD/SO4 2‚àí ratio reaches 0.5. Model simulation suggests that an optimal granule diameter for the maximum methane production efficiency can be achieved while the sulfate reduction efficiency is not significantly affected by variation in granule size. It also indicates that the methane production and sulfate reduction can be affected by ethanol and sulfate loading rates, and the microbial community development stage in the reactor, which provided comprehensive insights into the system for its practical operation.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Lack of ethanol-specific mathematical model: Previous models for anaerobic granular sludge reactors mainly focused on methane production and sulfate reduction using acetate and hydrogen as substrates. No model had specifically addressed ethanol as a key electron donor and carbon source in such systems.
  • Uncharacterized competition between SRB and FB for ethanol: The competition between sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) and fermentative bacteria (FB) for ethanol utilization had not been elucidated by previous models, which is critical for understanding the overall electron flow in the system.
  • Substrate penetration and microbial distribution in granules: In anaerobic granular sludge systems, differences in substrate penetration depth significantly affect the spatial distribution of different microbial populations, which needed to be incorporated into the model.
  • Comprehensive understanding of system performance: There was a need to quantitatively understand how operational parameters (granule size, ethanol loading rate (ELR), sulfate loading rate (SLR), and microbial community development stage) affect methane production and sulfate reduction efficiency in UASB reactors treating ethanol- and sulfate-containing wastewater.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Incorporation of acetate-utilizing SRB (ASRB) dynamics: The model showed that ASRB did not develop significantly during the study period due to their low growth rate and low initial abundance. Future work could focus on modeling the long-term competition between ASRB and acetate-utilizing MA (AMA) under prolonged high-sulfate conditions, as the model predicted ASRB would eventually outcompete AMA after extended operation.
  • pH dynamics and inhibition: The model did not include pH as a dynamic variable; the reactor performance failure caused by pH drops (Day 75‚Äì85 in Study Case I) could not be captured. Incorporating dynamic pH modeling and its inhibitory effects on microbial activities would improve model accuracy.
  • Differentiated H2SH_2SH2‚ÄãS inhibition constants: The current model used the same inhibition constant for all SRB and the same for all MA. When H2SH_2SH2‚ÄãS concentrations are high, applying distinct inhibition constants for each microbial group would improve model predictions.
  • Temperature effects: The model was calibrated at 35¬∞C. Extending the model to include temperature-dependent kinetics would broaden its applicability to different operational conditions.
  • Granule size distribution and dynamics: The model used an average granule diameter. Incorporating a dynamic granule size distribution model (granulation, breakage, and washout) would better reflect real UASB reactor behavior.
  • Other fermentable substrates: The model focused on ethanol as the key electron donor. Extending the model to include other fermentable compounds (e.g., fatty acids, sugars, amino acids) would make it more applicable to complex industrial wastewaters.
  • Real industrial wastewater validation: The model was validated using synthetic wastewater. Testing the model with real industrial wastewater (e.g., from brewery or pharmaceutical factories) with more complex compositions would further verify its practical applicability.
  • Optimization of inoculum composition: The modeling results suggested that high ASRB abundance in the inoculated sludge is preferred for sulfate removal. Future experimental studies could investigate strategies to enrich ASRB in inoculum sludge.
  • Upflow velocity and granule size control: The paper suggested that increasing upflow velocity could reduce granule size to enhance sulfate reduction. A coupled hydrodynamic-biofilm model could be developed to optimize this operational strategy.
  • Microbial community structure validation: The simulated stratified microbial distribution was not directly validated by experimental measurements (e.g., FISH or 16S rRNA sequencing with spatial resolution). Future work combining modeling with advanced molecular biology techniques would strengthen the model's biological basis.

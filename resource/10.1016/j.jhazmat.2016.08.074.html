Mathematical modeling of simultaneous carbon-nitrogen-sulfur removal from industrial wastewater

DOI: 10.1016/j.jhazmat.2016.08.074

Authors: Xi-Jun Xu, Chuan Chen, Ai-Jie Wang, Bing-Jie Ni, Wan-Qian Guo, Ye Yuan, Cong Huang, Xu Zhou, Dong-Hai Wu, Duu-Jong Lee, Nan-Qi Ren

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

A mathematical model of carbon, nitrogen and sulfur removal (C-N-S) from industrial wastewater was constructed considering the interactions of sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB), sulfide-oxidizing bacteria (SOB), nitrate-reducing bacteria (NRB), facultative bacteria (FB), and methane producing archaea (MPA). For the kinetic network, the bioconversion of C-N by heterotrophic denitrifiers (NO3- -> NO2- -> N2), and that of C-S by SRB (SO42- -> S2-) and SOB (S2- -> S0) was proposed and calibrated based on batch experimental data. The model closely predicted the profiles of nitrate, nitrite, sulfate, sulfide, lactate, acetate, methane and oxygen under both anaerobic and micro-aerobic conditions. The best-fit kinetic parameters had small 95% confidence regions with mean values approximately at the center. The model was further validated using independent data sets generated under different operating conditions. This work was the first successful mathematical modeling of simultaneous C-N-S removal from industrial wastewater and more importantly, the proposed model was proven feasible to simulate other relevant processes, such as sulfate-reducing, sulfide-oxidizing process (SR-SO) and denitrifying sulfide removal (DSR) process. The model developed is expected to enhance our ability to predict the treatment of carbon-nitrogen-sulfur contaminated industrial wastewater.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Lack of integrated mathematical model for simultaneous C-N-S removal: Although biological simultaneous removal of carbon (C), nitrogen (N), and sulfur (S) from industrial wastewater has been widely studied, few efforts had been devoted to mathematically modeling the integrated process, especially the synergistic and competitive relationships among multiple microbial groups.
  • Complex microbial interactions not captured: Industrial wastewater often contains co-contaminants of nitrate and sulfate. The interactions among sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB), sulfide-oxidizing bacteria (SOB), nitrate-reducing bacteria (NRB), facultative bacteria (FB), and methane-producing archaea (MPA) are complex and had not been comprehensively modeled in a single framework.
  • Competitive inhibition kinetics between SRB and NRB not quantified: The competitive inhibition coefficients (KI,NO3SRBK_{I,NO_3}^{SRB}KI,NO3‚ÄãSRB‚Äã and KI,SO4NRBK_{I,SO_4}^{NRB}KI,SO4‚ÄãNRB‚Äã) governing the competition between SRB and NRB for electron donors had not been well estimated and reported.
  • No unified simulation tool for C-N-S dynamics: Existing models (ASMs for C-N removal, ADM1 extensions for sulfate reduction) were developed separately and could not simulate the full C-N-S removal process including oxygen/nitrate-driven sulfide oxidation under both anaerobic and micro-aerobic conditions.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Incorporation of hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis: The current model does not consider methane production from H2H_2H2‚Äã by hydrogenotrophic methanogens. Future work could include this pathway when more information on H2H_2H2‚Äã consumption becomes available, making the model more complete for anaerobic digestion scenarios.
  • Inclusion of sulfate precipitation: The model ignores the occurrence of chemical precipitation in the presence of SO42‚àíSO_4^{2-}SO42‚àí‚Äã in real wastewater (e.g., formation of metal sulfide precipitates). Incorporating precipitation reactions would improve the model's applicability to real industrial wastewater.
  • More complex and realistic carbon sources: The current model uses lactate as the sole carbon source to simplify carbon metabolism. Real industrial wastewater contains much more complex organic carbon constituents. Future work should calibrate and validate the model with diverse carbon sources representative of large-scale real systems.
  • Long-term continuous reactor studies: The model was calibrated and validated using batch experimental data. Long-term studies in continuous reactors (e.g., EGSB, CSTR) under varying operating conditions would further strengthen model reliability and applicability.
  • Full-scale application and parameter uncertainty reduction: The model may not yet serve as a precise quantitative predictor in full-scale C-N-S removal applications due to parameter value uncertainty. More extensive calibration datasets from full-scale systems are needed.
  • Extension to complete sulfide oxidation to sulfate: The current model ignores SOB-mediated sulfide oxidation to sulfate (S2‚àí‚ÜíSO42‚àíS^{2-} \rightarrow SO_4^{2-}S2‚àí‚ÜíSO42‚àí‚Äã) under electron acceptor-limited conditions. Under excess oxygen conditions, this pathway may become significant and should be incorporated.
  • Culture-specific competitive inhibition parameters: The weak-overlap confidence regions for KI,NO3SRBK_{I,NO_3}^{SRB}KI,NO3‚ÄãSRB‚Äã and KI,SO4NRBK_{I,SO_4}^{NRB}KI,SO4‚ÄãNRB‚Äã between Culture 1 and Culture 2 suggest these parameters are culture-specific. Further investigation into the mechanisms driving this variability and development of more generalizable parameter estimation approaches is warranted.
  • Nitrate reduction by SRB: Ammonium production was not detected in this study, so nitrate use by SRB was excluded. However, some SRB are capable of reducing nitrate to ammonium (DNRA), and this pathway could be explored under different conditions.
  • Spatial heterogeneity and biofilm modeling: The current model assumes a well-mixed system. Extending the model to account for spatial gradients in biofilm or granular sludge systems (e.g., EGSB reactors) would improve its predictive capability for real reactor configurations.
  • Integration with the SDD (simultaneous desulfurization and denitrification) process optimization: While the model was shown to be applicable to SR-SO and DSR processes, a more detailed optimization study of the SDD process using the model could provide practical guidance for reactor design and operation.

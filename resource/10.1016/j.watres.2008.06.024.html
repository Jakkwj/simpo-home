Growth, maintenance and product formation of autotrophs in activated sludge: Taking the nitrite-oxidizing bacteria as an example

DOI: 10.1016/j.watres.2008.06.024

Authors: Bing-Jie Ni, Fang Fang, Wen-Ming Xie, Han-Qing Yu

The autotrophs in activated sludge play an important role in biological wastewater treatment, especially in the nitrification process. Compared with the heterotrophs in activated sludge, information about the growth, maintenance, and product formation of the autotrophs is still sparse. In this work both experimental and modeling approaches are used to investigate the growth, nitrite inhibition, maintenance, and formation of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) and soluble microbial products (SMP) of the autotrophs, with nitrite-oxidizing bacteria (NOB) in activated sludge as an example. The unified theory for EPS and SMP is integrated into our model to describe the microbial product formation of the NOB. Extensive experiments were carried out using the NOB-enriched in a sequencing batch reactor for the calibration and validation of the developed model. Results show that the NOB spend a considerable amount of energy on maintenance processes. Their apparent growth yield is estimated to be 0.044 mg COD biomass mg‚Åª¬π N. The model simulations reveal that the concentrations of EPS and SMP in the NOB-enriched culture initially increase, but later decrease gradually, and that the SMP formed in the nitrite oxidation process are biodegradable.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Lack of knowledge on autotroph behavior in activated sludge: Compared to heterotrophs, information about the growth, maintenance, and product formation of autotrophs (especially nitrite-oxidizing bacteria, NOB) in activated sludge is still sparse.
  • Incomplete existing models for nitrite oxidation: Published models concerning nitrite oxidation (e.g., Koch et al., 2000; Jubany et al., 2005) describe inhibitory influences of ammonia and nitrous acid, but do not account for:
    • Organic carbon inhibition
    • Biomass maintenance energy requirements
    • Microbial product formation (EPS and SMP) of NOB
  • Substrate inhibition at high nitrite concentrations: At high initial substrate concentrations, a model approach needs the inclusion of substrate (nitrite) inhibition using a Haldane-type equation.
  • Understanding EPS and SMP dynamics of autotrophs: The formation, hydrolysis, and biodegradability of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) and soluble microbial products (SMP) in NOB-enriched cultures have not been previously characterized or modeled.
  • Quantification of maintenance energy for NOB: The maintenance energy requirement of autotrophs is poorly understood, and its impact on apparent growth yield (YN,appY_{N,app}YN,app‚Äã) needs to be quantified.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • BAP source mechanisms: The paper adopts the Laspidou and Rittmann (2002a,b) unified theory, which assumes EPS hydrolysis is the only source of BAP. However, as noted by Aquino and Stuckey (2008), cell lysis products and turnover of intracellular components may also contribute to BAP. Future work could develop more comprehensive models that account for multiple BAP sources in autotrophic systems.
  • Extension to other autotrophs: This study uses NOB as a representative autotroph. The model framework could be extended and validated for ammonium-oxidizing bacteria (AOB) and other autotrophs (e.g., anammox bacteria) to provide a more complete picture of autotrophic processes in biological nitrogen removal.
  • Effect of varying environmental conditions: The experiments were conducted at a fixed temperature (22¬∞C) and pH (7.3). The influence of temperature, pH, and dissolved oxygen on EPS/SMP formation, maintenance energy, and apparent growth yield of NOB could be investigated further.
  • Long-term continuous reactor simulations: The current model was validated in batch experiments. Applying and validating the model in continuous-flow systems (e.g., full-scale WWTPs) under dynamic loading conditions would be an important next step.
  • Nitrogen content of EPS and SMP: The paper simplifies the model by not accounting for organic nitrogen in biomass, EPS, and SMP. A more detailed model incorporating nitrogen cycling through these fractions could improve accuracy in nitrogen mass balance predictions.
  • Apparent growth yield variability: The paper notes that YN,appY_{N,app}YN,app‚Äã varies with sludge retention time (SRT) and specific growth rate. A systematic study of how SRT and operational conditions affect YN,appY_{N,app}YN,app‚Äã and maintenance energy of NOB would be valuable for practical design.
  • Interaction between NOB and heterotrophs: The heterotrophic population in this study grows only on SMP (UAP and BAP). The competition and interaction between NOB and heterotrophs under varying SMP concentrations and substrate conditions could be explored in more detail.
  • Validation of the unified EPS/SMP theory for autotrophs: There are disagreements in the literature about the unified theory (Aquino and Stuckey, 2008; Ramesh et al., 2006). More experimental work is needed to clarify the mechanisms of BAP formation specifically for autotrophic microorganisms.
  • Inhibition by free nitrous acid (FNA) vs. nitrite: The paper expresses inhibition in terms of nitrite concentration (KI,NO2K_{I,NO_2}KI,NO2‚Äã‚Äã) since pH and temperature are constant. Future studies could decouple FNA and nitrite inhibition under varying pH and temperature conditions.

Gemini 3.1 Pro:

  • Further investigation into the exact sources and formation mechanisms of biomass-associated products (BAP), as there are controversial conclusions in the literature (e.g., whether BAP originates solely from EPS hydrolysis or also from cell lysis and soluble intracellular components).
  • Exploring the variation of the apparent growth yield of NOB under different growth conditions, such as varying sludge retention times (SRT) and specific growth rates in full-scale wastewater treatment plants.
  • Extending the model to include other autotrophic populations, such as ammonia-oxidizing bacteria (AOB), to create a complete two-step nitrification model with EPS and SMP formation.

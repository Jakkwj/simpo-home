Mathematical modeling of autotrophic denitriÔ¨Åcation (AD) process with sulphide as electron donor

Autotrophic denitrification (AD) plays a critical role in nitrate removal from organic carbon-deficient wastewaters with a high level of nitrogen oxides. However, the AD process is not included in the current denitrification models, which limits the application of AD technology for wastewater treatment. In this work, a kinetic model for AD process involved 4 processes and 5 components with 9 parameters is established to describe the sulphide biooxidation and nitrite removal process. In this model, 4 oxidation‚Äìreduction reactions using sulphide as electronic donor in the AD process are taken into account. The model parameters are optimized by fitting data from the experiments with different combinations of sulphide, sulphur, sulphate, nitrate and nitrite at various concentrations. Model calibration and validation results demonstrate that the developed model is able to reasonably describe the removal rates of nitrate, nitrite, sulphide and sulphur in the AD process. The model simulation results also show that the sulphur term (Œ∑(S)) in the kinetic equations of nitrate, nitrite, sulphur and sulphate remains constant, rather than being controlled by its own concentration. Furthermore, with this model the products of sulphide biooxidation in the AD process, sulphur and sulphate, and their concentrations can be accurately predicted. Therefore, this model provides a strategy to control the sulphate concentration below the discharge limits or recover sulphur as the main end product from sulphide biooxidation.

  • Incorporation of inhibition effects: This study excluded inhibition of NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã and S2‚àíS^{2-}S2‚àí on sulphide oxidation because concentrations were below inhibitory thresholds (>800 mg/L for NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã, >500 mg/L for S2‚àíS^{2-}S2‚àí).   • Mechanistic understanding of Œ∑(S)\eta(S)Œ∑(S): The paper found that Œ∑(S)\eta(S)Œ∑(S) is best described as a constant rather than a Monod-type function, suggesting it is controlled by S0S^0S0 particle morphology, size, and related enzymes rather than concentration.   • Extension to continuous flow reactors: The model was developed and validated using SBR batch data. Its applicability to continuous flow systems (e.g., CSTR, fixed-bed, biofilm reactors) should be tested and potentially adapted.

  • Integration with ASM framework: The current model is standalone. Integrating it into the broader ASM (Activated Sludge Model) framework would allow simultaneous simulation of heterotrophic and autotrophic denitrification in mixed systems, which is common in real wastewater treatment plants.

  • Temperature and pH effects on kinetic parameters: The model was developed at a fixed temperature (30¬∞C30¬∞C30¬∞C) and pH (~7.2). Temperature and pH correction factors (e.g., Arrhenius-type) should be incorporated to extend the model's applicability to varying environmental conditions, as cross-effects of temperature, pH, and free ammonia on AD have been reported.

  • Microbial community dynamics: The model uses a lumped ADB biomass (XADX_{AD}XAD‚Äã) without distinguishing between different sulphur-oxidizing denitrifying species. Incorporating microbial community structure and population dynamics could improve model accuracy and mechanistic insight.

  • Real-time monitoring and control strategy: The paper suggests using the model to control SO42‚àíSO_4^{2-}SO42‚àí‚Äã below discharge limits or recover S0S^0S0. A practical real-time control algorithm based on model predictions needs to be developed and tested, especially since in-situ S0S^0S0 measurement remains challenging.

  • Thiosulphate (S2O32‚àíS_2O_3^{2-}S2‚ÄãO32‚àí‚Äã) and other sulphur intermediates: This study excluded thiosulphate and sulphite as they were identified as chemical oxidation intermediates rather than biodenitrification intermediates.

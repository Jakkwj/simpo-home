Modelling nitrification, heterotrophic growth and predation in activated sludge

DOI: 10.1016/j.watres.2005.09.038

A mathematical model describing the interaction between nitrifiers, heterotrophs and predators in wastewater treatment has been developed. The inclusion of a predation mechanism is a new addition to the existing activated sludge models. The developed model considered multi-substrate consumption and multi-species growth, maintenance and decay in a culture where nitrifiers, heterotrophs and predators (protozoa and metazoa) are coexisting. Two laboratory-scale sequenced batch reactors (SBRs) operated at different sludge retention time (SRT) of 30 and 100 days for a period of 4 years were used to calibrate and validate the model. Moreover, to assess the predator activity, a simple procedure was developed, based on measuring the respiration rate with and without the presence of the predators. The model successfully described the performance of two SBRs systems. The fraction of active biomass (ammonia oxidisers, nitrite oxidisers and heterotrophs) predicted by the proposed model was only 33% and 14% at SRT of 30 and 100 days, respectively. The high fraction of inert biomass predicted by the model was in accordance with the microscopic investigations of biomass viability in both reactors. The presented model was used to investigate the effect of increasing sludge age and the role of predators on the biomass composition of the tested SBR system.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Incomplete representation of microbial community in existing ASMs: Traditional activated sludge models (ASM1, ASM2, ASM2d, ASM3) treat bacteria as the sole active biomass, while lumping all other microbial activities (protozoa, metazoa, phage infection, predation) into a simplified "decay" process. This oversimplification ignores the explicit role of predators (protozoa and metazoa) in the system.
  • Lack of quantitative understanding of predator-prey interactions: Although predators are clearly present and stable in wastewater treatment plants, little mathematical modelling work had been done to explicitly describe the interaction between nitrifiers, heterotrophs, and predators (protozoa and metazoa) in activated sludge systems.
  • Inability to predict active biomass fraction accurately: Without a predation mechanism, existing models cannot accurately predict the fraction of active biomass (ammonia oxidisers XNH4X_{NH_4}XNH4‚Äã‚Äã, nitrite oxidisers XNO2X_{NO_2}XNO2‚Äã‚Äã, and heterotrophs XHX_HXH‚Äã) versus inert biomass (XIX_IXI‚Äã) at different sludge retention times (SRTs).
  • No standard method to assess predator activity: There was no simple, reliable procedure to quantitatively determine the contribution of predators to respiration in a mixed suspended culture.
  • Understanding the effect of SRT and predators on biomass composition: The role of sludge age and predators on the population structure (nitrifiers, heterotrophs, predators, inert biomass) in SBR systems was not well quantified.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Full-scale validation: The authors explicitly state that "the practical application of the developed model and assessments of predators activity needs to be verified under full-scale activated sludge plant operation." The model was only validated at laboratory scale with two SBRs.
  • Differentiation of predator types: In the current model, all predators (protozoa, rotifers, nematodes, mites) are lumped into a single component (XpredatorsX_{predators}Xpredators‚Äã). Future work could attempt to differentiate between different types of predators (e.g., flagellates, ciliates, metazoa) with their own kinetic and stoichiometric parameters, as the interactions between predators themselves are complex.
  • Selective inhibition of predators in practice: The use of NaCl as a selective inhibitor for predators under practical/full-scale conditions needs further investigation. Suitable dosage strategies and the effect of predator suppression on overall system performance (e.g., effluent quality, sludge settleability) should be carefully evaluated.
  • Soluble microbial products (SMP) by nitrifiers: The paper acknowledges that nitrifiers may produce SMPs that support heterotrophic populations (Rittman et al., 1994). Incorporating an SMP sub-model could improve the description of heterotrophic growth in autotrophic systems without relying on unrealistically high yield values for nitrifiers.
  • Extension to phosphorus removal systems: The current model focuses on nitrification and heterotrophic growth. Extending the predation model to biological phosphorus removal (EBPR) systems (e.g., PAOs, GAOs) would be valuable.
  • Predator kinetics under varying environmental conditions: Due to lack of data, predator behavior under different environmental conditions (temperature, pH, anoxic/anaerobic conditions) was simplified. More detailed experimental studies on predator kinetics are needed.
  • Application to membrane bioreactors (MBRs): The paper notes that systems operated at long SRT (such as MBRs) need careful optimization to avoid inert biomass accumulation. Applying and validating the model for MBR systems would be a natural extension.
  • Interaction between predators and biofilm systems: The model was developed for suspended growth systems. Extending it to biofilm or granular sludge systems where predator-prey dynamics may differ significantly would be of interest.
  • Quantitative live/dead differentiation: The LIVE/DEAD staining could not quantitatively differentiate between the two reactors (SRT 30 vs. 100 days). More advanced techniques (e.g., flow cytometry) could be explored for quantitative biomass viability assessment.
  • Impact of predators on effluent quality: The model currently focuses on biomass composition and oxidation rates. Linking predator activity to effluent suspended solids and turbidity (as studied by Curds, 1971) could provide a more complete picture of treatment performance.

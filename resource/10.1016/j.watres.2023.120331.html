Physics-informed neural network-based serial hybrid model capturing the hidden kinetics for sulfur-driven autotrophic denitrification process

DOI: 10.1016/j.watres.2023.120331

Authors: Xu Zou, Hongxiao Guo, Chukuan Jiang, Duc Viet Nguyen, Guang-Hao Chen, Di Wu

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

Sulfur-driven autotrophic denitrification (SdAD) is a biological process that can remove nitrate from low carbon/nitrogen (C/N) ratio wastewater. Although this process has been intensively researched, the mechanism whereby its intermediates (i.e., elemental sulfur and nitrite ions) are generated and accumulated remains elusive. Existing mathematical models developed for SdAD cannot accurately predict the intermediates in SdAD because of the incomplete knowledge of process kinetic resulting from changes in the environmental conditions and electron competition during SdAD. To address this limitation, we proposed a novel serial hybrid model structure based on a physics-informed neural network (PINN) to capture the dynamics of the process kinetics and predict the substrate concentrations in SdAD. In this study, we evaluated the model through numerical experiments and applied it to real case studies involving batch and continuous-flow reactor scenarios. By leveraging the PINN approach, the hybrid model yielded accurate predictions at both the state (i.e. substrate concentration) and kinetic levels in the numerical experiments and performed better than both mechanistic and purely data-driven models in the case studies. Furthermore, we used the trained hybrid model to design control strategies for SdAD and a novel integrated process involving SdAD and anammox for energy-efficient nitrogen removal. Finally, we discuss the advantages and application scope of the PINN-based hybrid model.

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

The paper addresses the following core challenges in modeling the sulfur-driven autotrophic denitrification (SdAD) process:

  • Incomplete mechanistic understanding of intermediates: In SdAD, obligatory intermediates such as biogenic elemental sulfur (Sbio0S^0_{bio}Sbio0‚Äã) and nitrite (NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã) tend to accumulate, but the mechanisms governing their generation and accumulation remain elusive due to complex environmental conditions and electron competition dynamics.

  • Limitations of existing mechanistic models: Traditional mechanistic models (e.g., Monod-type subprocess rate models, Haldane inhibition equations, and the indirect coupling of electrons (ICE) model) cannot accurately predict intermediate concentrations in SdAD because:

    • They rely on fixed kinetic parameters that cannot adapt to dynamic environmental conditions (e.g., pH, DO, ORP, temperature).
    • They fail to capture the full complexity of electron competition between multiple electron donors (H2SH_2SH2‚ÄãS, S0S^0S0) and acceptors (NO3‚àíNO_3^-NO3‚àí‚Äã, NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã, DO).
    • They perform poorly under high sulfide concentrations (>100 mg/L) in real wastewater treatment scenarios.

  • Limitations of purely data-driven models: While data-driven models can capture dynamic trends, they:

    • Require large, high-quality datasets that are difficult to obtain from real wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs).
    • Are prone to overfitting and cannot predict unmeasured intermediates (e.g., S0S^0S0).
    • Lack physical interpretability and cannot enforce mass/electron balance constraints.

  • Difficulty in training serial hybrid models: Although serial hybrid models are theoretically more suitable for learning hidden bioprocess kinetics than parallel hybrid models, their training has historically been limited by the difficulty of backpropagating through the mechanistic ODE component using conventional time-discrete methods.

In summary, the paper aims to develop a novel modeling framework that can accurately predict substrate concentrations and capture hidden kinetics in SdAD under dynamic real-world conditions, while maintaining physical interpretability and working with limited data.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

Based on the paper's discussion and conclusions, the following directions merit further investigation:

1. Opening the "Black Box" of Hidden Kinetics‚Äã

  • The hybrid model captures hidden kinetics within Net1, but the internal representations remain opaque. The authors suggest using deep symbolic regression to convert the learned functional kinetic parameters into explicit mathematical expressions, transforming the "black box" into a "white box" model. This would enable full mechanistic interpretation of the hidden dynamics.

2. Incorporating Microbiome Dynamics‚Äã

  • The current model does not account for changes in microbial community composition. For systems where the microbiome shifts over time (e.g., due to environmental perturbations or long-term operation), incorporating genetic/metagenomic information of bacteria as additional input features could improve model accuracy and generalizability.

3. Extension to More Complex Mechanistic Frameworks‚Äã

  • The paper notes that PINN is well-suited for solving partial differential equations (PDEs). Future work could integrate more complex mechanistic components such as:
    • Mass transfer models (e.g., biofilm diffusion-reaction models)
    • Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) for reactor hydrodynamics
    • This would enable modeling of spatially distributed systems like biofilm reactors more rigorously.

4. Application to Other Wastewater Treatment Processes‚Äã

  • The PINN-based serial hybrid model framework is not limited to SdAD. It could be applied to other processes with unclear mechanisms, including:
    • Hydrolysis in anaerobic digestion
    • Biomass decay kinetics
    • Sulfur-based denitrification/anammox (SDA) integrated processes
    • Nitrous oxide (N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO) production during denitrification

5. Validation of Model Interpretation Results‚Äã

  • The SHAP and PDP interpretation results (e.g., effects of ORP, pH, DO on denitrification rates) provide mechanistic insights but require further experimental validation to confirm their physical significance, particularly for the unusual peak observed at 75 h in Case 2.

6. Handling of Intermediate Species (NONONO, N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO)‚Äã

  • The current model assumes NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã is directly reduced to N2N_2N2‚Äã, neglecting NONONO and N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO intermediates. Future models should incorporate these gaseous intermediates, which are important greenhouse gases and may significantly affect nitrogen removal efficiency.

7. Real-Time Control and Online Learning‚Äã

  • The trained surrogate model (Net2) enables fast predictions suitable for real-time applications. Future work could explore online learning or adaptive model updating strategies to continuously refine the model as new operational data become available from WWTPs.

8. Scalability and Generalizability Across Different Systems‚Äã

  • The model was validated on one batch reactor dataset and one full-scale CSTR. Testing the model's transferability across different reactor configurations, scales, and wastewater compositions (e.g., different C/N ratios, salinities) would strengthen its practical applicability.

9. Balancing Functional and Constant Kinetic Parameters‚Äã

  • The authors suggest that by properly balancing the number of functional kinetic parameters and constant parameters, the serial hybrid model could be trained with data amounts comparable to those required for mechanistic models. A systematic study on parameter sensitivity and identifiability would be valuable.

10. Integration with Process Optimization and Digital Twins‚Äã

  • The trained hybrid model could serve as the core of a digital twin for SdAD-based WWTPs, enabling real-time process monitoring, fault detection, and optimization of operational parameters (e.g., HRT, recirculation ratio, DO setpoints).

Gemini 3.1 Pro:

  • Applying the PINN-based serial hybrid model to other wastewater treatment processes with unclear mechanisms, such as hydrolysis in anaerobic digestion and biomass decay.
  • Integrating more complicated mechanistic models, such as mass transfer and fluid dynamics, into the same model framework.
  • Using deep symbolic regression to fit the hidden kinetics captured by the neural network (Net1), thereby converting the black box model into a fully interpretable white box model.
  • Incorporating genetic information of bacteria into the prediction data for modeling scenarios where the microbiome might change.

Gemini 3.0 Pro:

  • Microbiome Integration: The current model does not account for changes in the microbiome. Future work could include genetic information of bacteria as prediction data.
  • "White Box" Conversion: The hidden kinetics are currently captured by a neural network (Net1). Future research could use deep symbolic regression to fit these captured kinetics into explicit mathematical equations, turning the "black box" into a "white box".
  • Mass Balance in Surrogate: The surrogate model (Net2) used for acceleration does not strictly enforce mass balance, which might lead to violations under certain extrapolation conditions (though the hybrid model itself does enforce it).
  • Data Requirements: While reduced compared to pure DL, the method still requires sufficient data coverage for training.

1. Background and Motivation‚Äã

  • SdAD is a promising biological process for removing reactive nitrogen from low C/N ratio wastewater, using reduced sulfur compounds (H2SH_2SH2‚ÄãS, S0S^0S0) as electron donors and NO3‚àíNO_3^-NO3‚àí‚Äã/NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã as electron acceptors.

  • Existing mechanistic models fail to accurately predict these intermediates, while purely data-driven models suffer from overfitting and require large datasets.

Quantifying sensitivity and uncertainty analysis of a new mathematical model for the evaluation of greenhouse gas emissions from membrane bioreactors

DOI: 10.1016/j.memsci.2014.10.008

Authors: Giorgio Mannina, Alida Cosenza

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

A new mathematical model able to quantify greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in terms of carbon dioxide (CO2) and nitrous oxide (N2O) for a Membrane Bioreactor (MBR) is presented. The proposed mathematical model is of the Activated Sludge Model (ASM) family and takes into account simultaneously both biological and physical processes (e.g., membrane fouling). An analysis of the key factors and sources of uncertainty influencing GHG emissions is also presented. Specifically, the standardized regression coefficient, the Extended-FAST and a Monte Carlo based method are employed for assessing model factors which influence three performance indicators: effluent quality index, operational costs and GHGs. Model factors are classified as important, non-influential and interacting. The model is applied to a University Cape Town‚ÄìMBR pilot plant which was object of an extensive field gathering campaign. The results reveal that model factors related to nitrogen transformation and membrane separation processes play a central role in the uncertainty of GHG estimation. Model factors that are associated with physical processes exhibit large first-order and total-order effects, which emphasises the importance of a holistic approach that jointly considers biological and physical processes. Furthermore, the membrane has a key role in GHG emissions as a result of the cake layer thickness which in turns influences the mass of substrate retained by the membrane and, thus, the biological process in the MBR. The results show that a modeller should not exclude the role of phosphorus in the contribution of accumulating organisms during the prediction of GHGs due to the high interaction of N2O. The results reveal that the uncertainty in the emission factors for CO2 is higher than the uncertainty in the emission factors for N2O (namely, 2.2 and 0.17%, respectively).

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

The paper addresses several interconnected problems in the field of wastewater treatment and environmental engineering:

  • Lack of GHG models for MBR systems: Prior to this study, mathematical models for quantifying greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (CO2CO_2CO2‚Äã and N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO) had only been developed and applied to conventional activated sludge (CAS) systems. No mechanistic model existed specifically for Membrane Bioreactor (MBR) systems.

  • Transferability limitations: MBR systems have unique characteristics (solid‚Äìliquid separation, high suspended solids concentrations, long sludge retention times, membrane fouling) that fundamentally differ from CAS systems, making direct transfer of CAS-based GHG models inappropriate.

  • Uncertainty in GHG emission factors: The fixed conversion factors used by the IPCC for estimating GHG emissions from WWTPs are highly uncertain, and empirical models based on single emission factors cannot reproduce the variability of GHG production related to changes in plant configuration, operating conditions, and influent variability.

  • Absence of sensitivity and uncertainty analysis for MBR GHG models: Although sensitivity and uncertainty analyses had been applied to CAS-based GHG models (e.g., Sweetapple et al. [11]), no such analysis had been performed for MBR systems, leaving key sources of uncertainty unidentified.

  • Need for a holistic modelling approach: Existing models did not simultaneously consider both biological processes (nitrification, denitrification, phosphorus removal) and physical processes (membrane fouling, cake layer formation) in the context of GHG emissions.

Q: What experiments were done for the paper?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

Pilot Plant Operation‚Äã

  • A UCT-MBR pilot plant was operated for 165 days, fed with 40 L h‚àí1^{-1}‚àí1 of real municipal wastewater.
  • The pilot plant configuration consisted of three reactors in series: anaerobic, anoxic, and aerobic tanks, plus an aerobic MBR tank with two submerged hollow fibre membrane modules (Zenon Zeeweed, ZW 10).
  • Recycled fluxes were maintained: from MBR tank to aerobic tank, from aerobic to anoxic, and from anoxic to anaerobic.
  • Until day 76: complete sludge retention (no withdrawal); after day 76: regular sludge withdrawal to maintain a sludge age of 37 days.

  • Samples were collected three times per week from six sections:
    • Section 0: Composite influent wastewater samples
    • Sections 1‚Äì4: Mixed liquor grab samples from anaerobic, anoxic, aerobic, and MBR tanks

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Inclusion of nitrifier denitrification (AOB denitrification pathway): The current ASM2d‚ÄìSMP‚ÄìGHG model deliberately excludes the nitrifier denitrification process due to model complexity and the lack of a validated mathematical approach. Future work could incorporate this pathway as better mechanistic understanding and measurement data become available.

  • Incorporation of CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã emissions: The model currently quantifies only CO2CO_2CO2‚Äã and N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO. Methane (CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã), another significant GHG from WWTPs, is not included. Extending the model to include CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã would provide a more complete GHG inventory.

  • Integration of GHG terms into the EQI: The results show that GHG-related model factors are non-influential for the EQI as currently defined. Including GHG contributions in the EQI expression would better reflect the total environmental impact of MBR systems.

  • Validation with measured GHG data: The model was calibrated and validated using liquid-phase water quality data, but not directly against measured CO2CO_2CO2‚Äã and N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO emission data. Future studies should use on-site GHG measurements to improve model accuracy and reduce uncertainty.

  • Application to full-scale MBR plants: The study was conducted on a pilot-scale UCT-MBR. Applying the model to full-scale systems would test its scalability and practical applicability.

  • Refined assessment of PAO-related model factors: The Extended-FAST results reveal high interaction effects from PAO activity parameters (qppq_{pp}qpp‚Äã, qfeq_{fe}qfe‚Äã, YPO4Y_{PO4}YPO4‚Äã) on N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO emissions.   • Improved characterisation of nitrogen transformation parameters: Parameters such as Œ∑g3\eta_{g3}Œ∑g3‚Äã, Œ∑g4\eta_{g4}Œ∑g4‚Äã, and KO2,HK_{O2,H}KO2,H‚Äã are identified as highly influential for N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO uncertainty.   • Exploration of different MBR configurations: The model was applied only to a UCT-MBR configuration. Other MBR configurations (e.g., submerged MBR with different reactor arrangements) could be investigated.

  • Seasonal and dynamic variability analysis: The influence of temperature-dependent parameters and seasonal influent variability on GHG emissions could be explored more systematically.

  • Energy-GHG trade-off optimisation: Since the membrane (CEC_ECE‚Äã) is identified as the dominant factor for both operational costs and GHG emissions, optimisation strategies that balance energy consumption (aeration, backwashing) against GHG reduction could be developed.

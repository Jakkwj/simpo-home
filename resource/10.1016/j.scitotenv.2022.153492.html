Expanding ASM models towards integrated processes for short-cut nitrogen removal and bioplastic recovery

DOI: 10.1016/j.scitotenv.2022.153492

Authors: Joao M. Ribeiro, Vincenzo Conca, Jorge M.M. Santos, Daniel F.C. Dias, Nilay Sayi-Ucar, Nicola Frison, Adrian Oehmen

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

In next-generation water resource recovery facilities (WRRFs), it is becoming increasingly important to save energy costs and promote resource recovery of valuable products. One way of reducing the substantial aeration energy costs at WRRFs is to employ shortcut N removal, while polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) production and recovery as bioplastic is a promising means of recovering a valuable product from biosolids. Both objectives can be achieved simultaneously through the Short-Cut Enhanced Phosphorus and PHA Recovery (SCEPPHAR) process. However, current mathematical models have not previously been employed to describe the behavior of such a process, which limits engineering design and optimisation of process operation. This work focusses on extending the ASM3 model towards the description of short-cut nitrogen removal and simultaneous PHA recovery in a sidestream treatment process. The calibrated and validated model described very well the nitritation process coupled with the aerobic feast/anoxic famine process for the selection of PHA producing organisms at a pilot-scale facility operated in Carbonera, Italy, where the normalised root mean squared error (NRMSE) was consistently < 20%. Furthermore, the model applied to the PHA selection stage could effectively describe the PHA accumulation stage without recalibration. A simulation study was performed using the modified ASM3 model to assess the relative benefits of the SCEPPHAR process strategy as compared to the fully aerobic selection process for mixed culture PHA production. While the level of PHA production was found to be 34% lower with SCEPPHAR, a 43% savings in volatile fatty acids (VFAs) demand, a 15% decrease in Total suspended solids (TSS) production and a 28% decrease in oxygen demand were also achievable, which could lead to savings in operational costs. This study facilitates the design and optimisation of WRRFs that integrate short-cut N removal with PHA production, saving aeration energy costs while achieving resource recovery.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

This paper attempts to solve the following key problems:

  • Lack of integrated mathematical models for next-generation WRRFs: Current Activated Sludge Models (ASMs) have not been previously employed to describe integrated processes that simultaneously achieve short-cut nitrogen removal and polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) bioplastic recovery, which limits engineering design and optimisation of such processes.
  • High energy consumption at WRRFs: Aeration accounts for 40‚Äì60% of total energy consumption at WRRFs. There is a need to reduce this through shortcut nitrogen removal (via-nitrite pathway, NO2‚àíNO_2^{-}NO2‚àí‚Äã-N shunt) instead of full nitrification/denitrification.
  • Resource recovery gap: While PHA production from mixed microbial cultures (MMC) is a promising means of recovering valuable bioplastics from biosolids, no ASM-type model had been developed to describe the combined SCEPPHAR (Short-Cut Enhanced Phosphorus and PHA Recovery) process integrating sidestream nitritation, aerobic feast/anoxic famine PHA selection, and fed-batch PHA accumulation.
  • Model transferability: There was no demonstration that a single calibrated parameter set could describe multiple distinct process stages (nitritation SBR, PHA-biomass selection SBR, and fed-batch PHA accumulation bioreactor) without recalibration.
  • Process comparison: No quantitative model-based comparison existed between the SCEPPHAR strategy (anoxic famine with NO2‚àíNO_2^{-}NO2‚àí‚Äã-N as electron acceptor) and the conventional fully aerobic MMC PHA production process coupled with conventional nitrification/denitrification.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

Based on the paper's discussion and conclusions, the following points can be explored further:

  • Economic assessment of process options: The authors explicitly suggest that future work should focus on applying the present model towards a comprehensive economic assessment of different WRRF process options for wastewater treatment and resource recovery, building on existing economic analyses of SCEPPHAR (Conca et al., 2020) and conventional PHA processes (Crutchik et al., 2020).

  • Optimisation of the nitritation process in SCEPPHAR: The paper notes that optimisation of the nitritation process in Scenario A would be beneficial to improve the percentage of sidestream N removal achieved by the SCEPPHAR process, since incomplete conversion of NH4+NH_4^+NH4+‚Äã-N to NO2‚àíNO_2^{-}NO2‚àí‚Äã-N reduces overall N removal efficiency.

  • Integration with mainstream WRRF modelling: The current model focuses on the sidestream SCEPPHAR process. Extending the model to describe the full WRRF, including the interaction between sidestream and mainstream treatment, would be a valuable next step.

  • Incorporation of phosphorus recovery: The SCEPPHAR process is named "Short-Cut Enhanced Phosphorus and PHA Recovery," yet the phosphorus recovery aspect was not fully modelled in this study. Future work could extend the model to describe enhanced biological phosphorus removal (EBPR) and phosphorus recovery (e.g., struvite precipitation).

  • Model application to different feedstocks and scales: The model was calibrated and validated with a specific pilot-scale system using anaerobic reject water and fermented cellulosic primary sludge. Validation with different feedstocks (e.g., other VFA sources, different wastewater compositions) and at full scale would increase model generalisability.

  • Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis: A formal sensitivity and uncertainty analysis of the model parameters (particularly frmax,STOfr_{max,STO}frmax,STO‚Äã, ŒºAOB\mu_{AOB}ŒºAOB‚Äã, YH,NO2Y_{H,NO2}YH,NO2‚Äã) would help identify the most influential parameters and quantify prediction uncertainty for design purposes.

  • PHA polymer composition modelling: The current model does not distinguish between different PHA monomers (3HB vs. 3HV). Extending the model to predict PHA composition as a function of VFA feed composition would be valuable for downstream processing and product quality control.

  • Carbon footprint and life cycle assessment (LCA): Given the lower oxygen demand and reduced sludge production of the SCEPPHAR process, a full LCA integrated with the model would quantify the environmental benefits more comprehensively.

  • Dynamic control strategies: The model could be used to develop and test advanced control strategies (e.g., real-time control of DO, SRT, feast/famine ratio) to optimise PHA yield and nitrogen removal simultaneously.

Q: Summarize the main points of the paper.

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

This paper presents the extension, calibration, validation, and application of the ASM3 model to describe the integrated SCEPPHAR (Short-Cut Enhanced Phosphorus and PHA Recovery) process for simultaneous short-cut nitrogen removal and PHA bioplastic recovery in a sidestream wastewater treatment context. The main points are:

1. Model Extension

  • The ASM3 model was extended to incorporate: (i) two-step nitrification/denitrification (separating AOB and NOB, and NO2‚àíNO_2^{-}NO2‚àí‚Äã-N from NO3‚àíNO_3^{-}NO3‚àí‚Äã-N) from Kaelin et al. 2. Model Calibration

  • The model was calibrated against pilot-scale data from the nitritation SBR (Period A: 87 days) and PHA-biomass selection SBR (Period A: 59 days) at the Carbonera WWTP, Italy.
  • Calibrated ŒºAOB\mu_{AOB}ŒºAOB‚Äã = 0.6 d‚àí1^{-1}‚àí1 (consistent with sidestream nitritation literature); kSTOk_{STO}kSTO‚Äã = 7.2 d‚àí1^{-1}‚àí1; ŒºH\mu_HŒºH‚Äã = 2.4 d‚àí1^{-1}‚àí1; YH,NO2Y_{H,NO2}YH,NO2‚Äã = 0.25 gCOD¬∑gCOD‚àí1^{-1}‚àí1.
  • NRMSE for nitritation SBR calibration: 8.80% for NH4+NH_4^+NH4+‚Äã-N and 2.88% for NO2‚àíNO_2^{-}NO2‚àí‚Äã-N (well within the < 20% threshold).
  • NRMSE for PHA selection SBR calibration: 5.5% for VFAs, 13.9% for PHA, 13.9% for NO2‚àíNO_2^{-}NO2‚àí‚Äã-N, 3.0% for NH4+NH_4^+NH4+‚Äã-N.

3. Model Validation

  • The calibrated model was validated against independent cycle studies and long-term dynamic simulations (Period B: 112 days for nitritation SBR; 108 days for selection SBR), with NRMSE consistently < 20%.
  • Crucially, the same parameter set calibrated for the PHA-biomass selection SBR was successfully applied to the fed-batch PHA accumulation bioreactor without recalibration (NRMSE: 14.5% for acetate; 21.2% for VFAs), demonstrating model transferability across process stages.

4. Process Comparison via Simulation

  • Scenario A (SCEPPHAR: nitritation + anoxic famine PHA selection) vs. Scenario B (conventional: full nitrification/denitrification + aerobic famine PHA selection):
    • Scenario A advantages: 43% savings in VFA demand, 15% decrease in TSS production, 28% decrease in oxygen demand.
    • Scenario B advantages: 34% higher PHA production rate, 10% higher sidestream N removal rate.
  • The SCEPPHAR process offers significant operational cost savings through reduced aeration energy and VFA consumption, at the expense of lower PHA yield.

5. Conclusions

  • The modified ASM3 model is a valuable tool for designing and optimising next-generation WRRFs integrating short-cut N removal with PHA recovery.
  • The SCEPPHAR process is a promising, lower-cost, lower-carbon-footprint alternative to conventional aerobic MMC PHA production, and warrants further evaluation for full-scale WRRF integration.

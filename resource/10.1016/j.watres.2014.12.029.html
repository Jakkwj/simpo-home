Evaluating two concepts for the modelling of intermediates accumulation during biological denitrification in wastewater treatment

DOI: 10.1016/j.watres.2014.12.029

Authors: Yuting Pan, Bing-Jie Ni, Huijie Lu, Kartik Chandran, David Richardson, Zhiguo Yuan

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

The accumulation of the denitrification intermediates in wastewater treatment systems is highly undesirable, since both nitrite and nitric oxide (NO) are known to be toxic to bacteria, and nitrous oxide (N2O) is a potent greenhouse gas and an ozone depleting substance. To date, two distinct concepts for the modelling of denitrification have been proposed, which are represented by the Activated Sludge Model for Nitrogen (ASMN) and the Activated Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons (ASM-ICE), respectively. The two models are fundamentally different in describing the electron allocation among different steps of denitrification. In this study, the two models were examined and compared in their ability to predict the accumulation of denitrification intermediates reported in four different experimental datasets in literature. The N-oxide accumulation predicted by the ASM-ICE model was in good agreement with values measured in all four cases, while the ASMN model was only able to reproduce one of the four cases. The better performance of the ASM-ICE model is due to that it adopts an ‚Äúindirect coupling‚Äù modelling concept through electron carriers to link the carbon oxidation and the nitrogen reduction processes, which describes the electron competition well. The ASMN model, on the other hand, is inherently limited by its structural deficiency in assuming that carbon oxidation is always able to meet the electron demand by all denitrification steps, therefore discounting electron competition among these steps. ASM-ICE therefore offers a better tool for predicting and understanding intermediates accumulation in biological denitrification.

Q: How the paper solves this problem?

Gemini 3.0 Pro:

The paper solves this problem by evaluating and comparing two distinct mathematical modeling concepts against experimental data:

  • Model I (ASMN): The Activated Sludge Model for Nitrogen, which uses a "direct coupling approach" where carbon oxidation and nitrogen reduction are directly coupled in a single reaction. It assumes carbon oxidation always meets the electron demand.
  • Model II (ASM-ICE): The Activated Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons, which uses an "indirect coupling approach". It introduces electron carriers (MoxM_{ox}Mox‚Äã and MredM_{red}Mred‚Äã) to link carbon oxidation to nitrogen reduction, allowing for the simulation of electron competition when the electron supply rate is limiting.
  • The authors calibrated and validated these models using four distinct experimental datasets from literature covering pure cultures and mixed denitrifying cultures under various conditions.

Q: Summarize the main points of the paper.

Gemini 3.0 Pro:

  • Model Comparison: The study compared ASMN (direct coupling) and ASM-ICE (indirect coupling) for predicting denitrification intermediates.
  • Performance: ASM-ICE successfully predicted N-oxide accumulation in all four experimental cases. ASMN failed in three out of four cases, only succeeding in Case 4 where electron donor was in excess.

  • Deficiency of ASMN: ASMN is structurally deficient because it lumps carbon oxidation and nitrogen reduction into single steps, implicitly assuming electron supply is never limiting, which contradicts reality in many scenarios.
  • Conclusion: ASM-ICE is the preferred tool for predicting and understanding intermediate accumulation (NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã, NONONO, N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO) in biological denitrification.

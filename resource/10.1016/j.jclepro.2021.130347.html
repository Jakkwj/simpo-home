Model-based identification of the dominant N2O emission pathway in a full-scale activated sludge system

DOI: 10.1016/j.jclepro.2021.130347

Authors: Mojtaba Maktabifard, Kati Blomberg, Ewa Zaborowska, Anna Mikola, Jacek Mąkinia

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

Activated sludge models (ASMs), extended with an N2O emission module, are powerful tools to describe the operation of full-scale wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs). Specifically, such models can investigate the most contributive N2O production pathways and guide towards N2O and carbon footprint (CF) mitigation measures. A common practice is to develop and validate models using data from a single WWTP. In this study, a successfully validated model in one plant (Slupsk/Poland) was extrapolated to another full-scale WWTP (Viikinmäki/Finland). For this purpose, the previously developed ASM No. 2d with the N2O module was used. Moreover, the results of calibration and validation of that model were compared with those obtained on the basis of the ASM No. 3 with an N2O module. A novel, rigorous calibration protocol, based on the system engineering approach, was implemented to minimize the number of adjusted parameters without compromising the accuracy of model predictions. The validated model accurately predicted the behavior of the system in terms of the liquid N2O production in the bioreactor and gaseous N2O emissions. Model-based identification of N2O production pathways revealed the key role of heterotrophs duo to their high abundance in the microbial community. The N2O emission factor (EF) at the studied plant was found between 0.9 and 0.94% of the influent TN-load for the validation and calibration period, respectively. Based on the model predictions, it was estimated that the aerobic zones contributed to over 93% of the N2O emitted to the atmosphere, while the remaining portion (7%) resulted from the N2O liquid-gas transfer in the non-aerated zones. The difference between the predicted N2O EF and the empirical EF calculation would lead to almost 1000 tonnes of CO2 equivalent reduction of the annual CF of the plant, which highlights the importance of model applications in CF studies.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

What Problem Does This Paper Attempt to Solve?​

This paper addresses several interconnected challenges in the field of wastewater treatment and greenhouse gas (GHG) management:

  • Uncertainty in N₂O emission factors (EFs): Empirical EFs (e.g., the IPCC recommended 1.6% of influent TN-load) are widely used in carbon footprint (CF) assessments, but they may significantly overestimate or underestimate actual N₂O emissions from specific plants, leading to large uncertainties in CF calculations.

  • Limited transferability of N₂O models: Mechanistic activated sludge models (ASMs) extended with N₂O modules are typically calibrated and validated for a single specific WWTP. There is a lack of studies confirming whether a validated model from one plant can be directly applied (extrapolated) to another full-scale WWTP.

  • Over-parameterization and calibration complexity: Existing N₂O models are complex and over-parameterized, making reliable calibration and validation difficult, especially when the number of adjusted parameters is large.

  • Identification of dominant N₂O production pathways: The relative contributions of different N₂O production pathways — namely NH2OHNH_2OHNH2​OH oxidation by AOB, AOB denitrification, and heterotrophic denitrification (DHET) — remain plant-specific and poorly understood at full scale, particularly the role of heterotrophs in aerobic zones.

  • Carbon footprint implications: The difference between model-predicted N₂O EFs and empirically assumed EFs can translate into significant errors in annual CF calculations for WWTPs, with potential consequences for environmental reporting and mitigation strategies.

  • Accurately determining the N2ON_2ON2​O emission factor (EF) is difficult, and relying on empirical EFs often leads to high uncertainties and overestimations in calculating the total carbon footprint (CF) of WWTPs.

Q: How the paper solves this problem?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

How the Paper Solves This Problem​

The paper employs a systematic, multi-step modelling approach to address the identified problems:

1. Model Selection and Extrapolation​

  • The previously validated ASM No. 2d with an N₂O module (ASM2d-N₂O) (originally developed and validated at Słupsk WWTP, Poland by Zaborowska et al., 2019) was extrapolated to the full-scale Viikinmäki WWTP (Helsinki, Finland).
  • The model considers three N₂O production pathways: (i) NH2OHNH_2OHNH2​OH oxidation by AOB, (ii) AOB denitrification, and (iii) DHET (denitrifying heterotrophic bacteria) denitrification.

2. Novel Rigorous Calibration Protocol (System Engineering Approach)​

A five-step systematic calibration protocol was developed:

  • Step 1: Build the GPS-X layout of Viikinmäki WWTP.
  • Step 2: Implement the biokinetic model with initial parameters from Zaborowska et al. (2019).
  • Step 3: Preliminary calibration using the process engineering approach — adjusting 10 parameters adopted from the previous study.
  • Step 4: Local Sensitivity Analysis (LSA) using a one-variable-at-a-time (OAT) approach (perturbation of ±5%) to identify the most influential kinetic parameters. A correlation matrix was then constructed to eliminate highly correlated parameter pairs (|r| > 0.9), reducing the number of parameters to be adjusted.
  • Step 5: Final calibration using the system engineering approach — optimizing only the reduced set of 4 most influential and least correlated parameters (μAOB\mu_{AOB}μAOB​, μNOB\mu_{NOB}μNOB​, ηNO2,H\eta_{NO2,H}ηNO2,H​, ηN2O,H\eta_{N2O,H}ηN2O,H​) using the Nelder-Mead simplex optimizer in GPS-X.

3. Goodness-of-Fit Evaluation​

Model performance was evaluated using multiple criteria:

  • Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) — threshold: NSE > 0.3
  • Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) — threshold: RMSE < 0.5
  • Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
  • Janus coefficient (for validation, should be close to 1)

4. N₂O Emission Modelling​

  • Two N₂O emission sub-models were applied: air-stripping for aerobic compartments and saturation-induced liquid-gas transfer (KLaanoxicK_La_{anoxic}KL​aanoxic​ = 2 d⁻¹) for anoxic compartments.
  • The KLaK_LaKL​a for oxygen was estimated using the OTR (Oxygen Transfer Rate) method (Method 1), which was found to improve N₂O emission predictions compared to the superficial gas velocity method (Method 2).

5. Model Comparison and Application​

  • The calibrated ASM2d-N₂O model results were compared with those from the previously published ASM3-N₂O model (Blomberg et al., 2018) applied to the same dataset.
  • The validated model was applied to identify the dominant N₂O production pathways in each reactor zone and to calculate a plant-specific N₂O EF, which was then compared with the empirical IPCC EF to quantify the CF implications.

Q: What experiments were done for the paper?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

What Experiments Were Done for the Paper?​

The study relied on full-scale monitoring campaigns and computer simulations at Viikinmäki WWTP. The experimental and data collection activities included:

Full-Scale Monitoring Campaigns​

  • Two measurement campaigns were conducted at Viikinmäki WWTP:
    • Calibration period: 12 days of continuous monitoring
    • Validation period: 5 days of continuous monitoring
  • These datasets were originally collected and described by Blomberg et al. (2018) and were reused in this study.

Online Monitoring Parameters Measured​

The process monitoring was fully automated via online analyzers:

  • Temperature — continuous measurement
  • Alkalinity — via ADI 2045TI analyzer
  • Dissolved Oxygen (DO) — continuous measurement
  • Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD) — online measurement
  • Mixed Liquor Suspended Solids (MLSS) — via BTG RD-20/10 analyzer
  • NH4+NH_4^+NH4+​-N — via A-ISE sc analyzer
  • NO3−NO_3^-NO3−​-N — via Nitratax plus analyzer
  • Liquid N2ON_2ON2​O concentrations in the first (Z4) and last (Z6) aerobic compartments — via Unisense analyzer
  • Total gaseous N2ON_2ON2​O emissions in the exhaust channel — via Gasmet FTIR analyzer

Computer Simulation Experiments​

  • The computer model of Viikinmäki WWTP was built in GPS-X 8.0 (Hydromantis, Canada).
  • Hydrodynamic modelling: The dispersion coefficient (ELE_LEL​) and Peclet number (PePePe) were calculated to determine the number of equivalent tanks (N = 8) in the Tank-in-Series (TIS) model.
  • Local Sensitivity Analysis (LSA): Performed on 10 kinetic parameters with a perturbation value of ±5%, evaluating sensitivity coefficients (SijS_{ij}Sij​) for five target variables (NH4+NH_4^+NH4+​-N, NO3−NO_3^-NO3−​-N, alkalinity, liquid N2ON_2ON2​O, gaseous N2ON_2ON2​O).
  • Correlation matrix analysis: Computed for the most sensitive parameters to identify and eliminate highly correlated pairs (|r| > 0.9).
  • Preliminary calibration: Adjusting 10 parameters using the process engineering approach.
  • Final calibration: Optimizing 4 parameters (μAOB\mu_{AOB}μAOB​, μNOB\mu_{NOB}μNOB​, ηNO2,H\eta_{NO2,H}ηNO2,H​, ηN2O,H\eta_{N2O,H}ηN2O,H​) using the Nelder-Mead simplex method.
  • Model validation: Evaluated on the 5-day dataset using NSE, RMSE, MAE, and Janus coefficient.
  • Comparison of two KLaK_LaKL​a estimation methods (OTR-based vs. superficial gas velocity-based) for their effect on N₂O emission predictions.
  • N₂O pathway analysis: Continuous simulation of N₂O production, consumption, and emission rates in each reactor zone (Z1–Z6) to identify dominant pathways.

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

What Are the Points That Can Be Explored Further?​

Based on the findings and limitations discussed in the paper, several directions for future research are identified:

1. Validation of Dominant N₂O Pathway Identification via Molecular Biology​

  • The paper acknowledges that the identification of dominant N₂O production pathways is based solely on model predictions. Gene activity measurements (e.g., functional gene expression analysis for nirS, nirK, nosZ, amoA) are needed to experimentally confirm the actual contributions of AOB and DHET to N₂O production under specific operational conditions.

2. Role of DHET Denitrification in Aerobic Zones at Different DO Levels​

  • The paper found that DHET denitrification was the dominant N₂O production pathway even in aerobic zones (DO ≈ 3 mg O2O_2O2​/L). The actual role of DHET in aerobic N₂O production at varying DO concentrations should be investigated in depth in future studies.

3. Expansion of N₂O Monitoring to Other WWTP Elements​

  • The current model only predicted N₂O emissions from the activated sludge reactors. Secondary clarifiers and other WWTP elements (e.g., sludge treatment units, primary settlers) were not included. Future models should incorporate plant-wide N₂O emission accounting.

4. Broader Cross-Plant Model Transferability Studies​

  • This study attempted to transfer a validated model from one plant (Słupsk) to another (Viikinmäki) and found that direct transfer was not successful without re-calibration. More studies are needed to systematically evaluate the transferability of N₂O models across plants with different configurations, sizes, and operational conditions.

5. Optimization of Aeration Strategies for N₂O Mitigation​

  • The paper suggests that maintaining DO between 1 and 2 mg O2O_2O2​/L could minimize overall N₂O production. Future work should investigate optimal aeration control strategies (e.g., ABAC, intermittent aeration) and their effect on N₂O EFs through both modelling and full-scale experiments.

6. Global Sensitivity Analysis (GSA) Instead of LSA​

  • The paper used Local Sensitivity Analysis (LSA) combined with correlation matrix analysis. Future studies could employ Global Sensitivity Analysis (GSA) to better account for interactions between kinetic parameters simultaneously, which would provide more robust parameter identifiability results.

7. Long-Term and Multi-Season Monitoring​

  • The calibration and validation periods were relatively short (12 and 5 days). Long-term monitoring across different seasons (temperature variations) would improve model robustness and the reliability of EF estimates.

8. Integration with Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and Carbon Footprint Tools​

  • The paper highlights the significant difference between model-predicted EFs and empirical EFs (nearly 1000 tonnes CO2CO_2CO2​ equivalent annually). Future work should integrate mechanistic N₂O models directly into plant-wide LCA and CF calculation tools to replace empirical EFs with more accurate, site-specific values.

Gemini 3.1 Pro:

  • Validating the model predictions of dominant N2ON_2ON2​O production pathways using site-specific data on the actual contributions of AOB and DHET, which can be obtained by gene activity measurements.
  • Expanding the model to predict N2ON_2ON2​O emissions from other elements of the WWTP, such as secondary clarifiers, rather than just the activated sludge reactors.
  • Investigating the exact triggering operational and environmental conditions (like different DO levels) that govern N2ON_2ON2​O production by DHET denitrification in aerobic zones.

Q: Summarize the main points of the paper.

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

Summary of the Main Points of the Paper​

1. Study Objective​

The paper aimed to confirm the applicability and universality of an existing N₂O model (ASM2d-N₂O, validated at Słupsk WWTP, Poland) by extrapolating it to another full-scale WWTP — Viikinmäki WWTP (Helsinki, Finland, 1.1 million PE), and to identify the dominant N₂O production pathways.

2. Model Framework​

  • The ASM2d-N₂O model (extended from IWA ASM2d) was used, incorporating three N₂O production pathways:
    • NH2OHNH_2OHNH2​OH oxidation by AOB
    • AOB denitrification
    • DHET (denitrifying heterotrophic bacteria) denitrification
  • The model was implemented in GPS-X 8.0 simulation platform.
  • The bioreactor was modelled as a Tank-in-Series (TIS) with N = 8 equivalent tanks.

3. Novel Calibration Protocol​

  • Direct transfer of the validated model from Słupsk to Viikinmäki was not successful (NSE < 0 for liquid N₂O).
  • A novel system engineering approach was developed: combining LSA and correlation matrix analysis to reduce the number of calibrated parameters from 10 to 4 key parameters (μAOB\mu_{AOB}μAOB​, μNOB\mu_{NOB}μNOB​, ηNO2,H\eta_{NO2,H}ηNO2,H​, ηN2O,H\eta_{N2O,H}ηN2O,H​).
  • The final calibration achieved satisfactory goodness-of-fit (NSE = 0.55 for NH4+NH_4^+NH4+​-N, NSE = 0.35 for liquid N₂O, NSE = 0.33 for N₂O emissions).

4. N₂O Emission Factor​

  • The model-predicted N₂O EF was 0.94% of the influent TN-load (calibration) and 0.9% (validation).
  • This is significantly lower than the IPCC (2019) empirical EF of 1.6%, resulting in a difference of approximately 10 kg N₂O/d and an annual CF overestimation of ~952 tonnes CO2CO_2CO2​ equivalent if the empirical EF is used.

5. Dominant N₂O Production Pathways​

  • DHET denitrification was the dominant N₂O production pathway in both anoxic and aerobic compartments, driven by the high biomass concentration of DHET (1295 mg COD/L vs. 78 mg COD/L for AOB).
  • In anoxic zones, DHET acted as a net N₂O sink (>99% of produced N₂O was consumed).
  • In aerobic zones, NH2OHNH_2OHNH2​OH oxidation contributed ~20% of N₂O production.
  • AOB denitrification contribution was negligible (< 2% in aerobic zones, max 3.3% in the last anoxic zone).

6. Spatial Distribution of N₂O Emissions​

  • Aerobic zones contributed >93% of total N₂O emitted to the atmosphere.
  • The remaining 7% resulted from N₂O liquid-gas transfer in non-aerated (anoxic) zones.

7. Model Comparison​

  • The ASM2d-N₂O model (this study) showed improved N₂O emission predictions compared to the ASM3-N₂O model (Blomberg et al., 2018), primarily due to the use of the OTR-based KLaK_LaKL​a estimation method and direct stripping calculation in GPS-X.

8. Practical Implications​

  • Mechanistic models provide more accurate, plant-specific N₂O EFs compared to empirical approaches, which is critical for reliable CF assessments of WWTPs.
  • The calibration protocol developed in this study can be applied to future modelling studies to minimize over-parameterization while maintaining prediction accuracy.

Gemini 3.1 Pro:

  • The ASM2d-N2ON_2ON2​O model was successfully extrapolated and calibrated for the Viikinmäki WWTP using a novel system engineering approach, reducing the adjusted kinetic parameters to only four.
  • The re-calibrated model accurately predicted both liquid N2ON_2ON2​O behavior and gaseous N2ON_2ON2​O emissions.
  • Heterotrophic denitrification (DHET) was identified as the dominant pathway for N2ON_2ON2​O production and consumption across all compartments, while the AOB denitrification pathway was negligible.
  • Aerobic zones contributed to over 93% of the emitted N2ON_2ON2​O.
  • The estimated N2ON_2ON2​O EF was 0.94% of the influent TN-load, which is lower than the IPCC empirical average (1.6%). Using the model-predicted EF instead of the empirical one results in a reduction of almost 1000 tonnes of CO2CO_2CO2​ equivalent in the plant's annual carbon footprint.

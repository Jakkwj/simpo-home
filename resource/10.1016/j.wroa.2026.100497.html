The crucial role of activated sludge models (ASMs) on wastewater treatment processes: Developments, applications, and future perspectives

DOI: 10.1016/j.wroa.2026.100497

Authors: Tianlong Zheng, Yuxin Wang, Lin Li, Junxin Liu, Pengyu Li

  • Abstract
  • Objective
  • Solution
  • Experiment
  • Exploration
  • Summary
  • Related

Activated sludge models (ASMs), the most widely used mathematical models for biological wastewater treatment, offer a simplified matrix-based representation of pollutant biochemical degradation. As understanding of wastewater treatment mechanisms has advanced, the simplifying assumptions of general ASMs have proven unreasonable under certain conditions, prompting their improvement. Existing reviews often focus on the specific application of ASMs, with limited comprehensive analyses of their multi-dimensional extensions and cross-model integrations. This review provides the first systematic overview of the latest developments in ASMs, focusing on model mechanism extension and multi-scale model integration. In terms of mechanism extension, the incorporation of new theories and secondary reaction has enhanced the accuracy of models in simulating membrane bioreactor systems, phosphorus removal, and industrial wastewater treatment. It has also quantified the generation and dissipation pathways of N2O and provided a basis for sludge reduction and sedimentation control. Regarding model integration, this review focuses on the coupling interfaces between ASMs and other models, such as anaerobic reaction models, convection-diffusion theory, hydrodynamic models, and machine learning. These coupled models enable full-scale simulation from micro-level biochemical reactions to macro-level environmental dynamics. Finally, the review emphasizes that future ASMs developments should focus on improving mechanisms and addressing emerging contaminants. It highlights that integrating artificial intelligence can serve as a key tool to balance model accuracy and parameter identifiability. The present review aims to establish a systematic research framework for ASMs, analyze the limitations of existing models, and ultimately provide insights for enhancing the precision and application of ASMs in wastewater treatment.

Q: What problem does this paper attempt to solve?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

  • Fragmentation of ASM research: Existing literature on ASMs is fragmented, with most reviews focusing on specific processes (e.g., MBR, emerging contaminants) rather than providing a comprehensive, systematic overview of the multi-dimensional extensions and cross-model integrations of ASMs.
  • Limitations of general ASMs: The simplifying assumptions embedded in standard ASMs (ASM1, ASM2d, ASM3) have proven unreasonable under certain conditions, such as:
    • Inability to accurately simulate MBR systems due to SMP and EPS accumulation
    • Incomplete representation of phosphorus metabolism (e.g., PAO vs. DPAO pathways, glycogen roles)
    • Lack of N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO and CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã emission quantification modules
    • Inapplicability to industrial wastewater with inhibitory compounds
    • Oversimplified hydraulic assumptions (ideal CSTR approximation)
  • Gap between mechanistic models and real-time applications: Mechanism-based ASMs are mostly confined to offline analysis due to complex parameter identification and calibration challenges, limiting their use in dynamic, real-time wastewater treatment optimization.
  • Lack of systematic framework: There is no comprehensive review that simultaneously addresses mechanistic supplementation, coupled model applications, and future scenario expansion of ASMs in wastewater treatment.

Q: What experiments were done for the paper?

Q: What are the points that can be explored further?

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

Based on the review's future perspectives section and identified limitations, the following points warrant further exploration:

1. Updating and Refining Model Theory

  • Introducing two-step nitrification-denitrification theory into ASMs to accurately predict NO2‚àíNO_2^-NO2‚àí‚Äã accumulation and N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO production under insufficient DO or high-temperature conditions
  • Developing reactive secondary sedimentation tank (SST) models that simultaneously describe physical sedimentation-compaction and biological denitrification processes
  • Incorporating temperature as a dynamic variable in rate equations to simulate microbial response to temperature fluctuations
  • Modeling anaerobic ammonia oxidation (ANAMMOX), SND, and GAC filtration processes using extended ASMs
  • Integrating omics data (metagenomics, metabolomics) with ASMs to link microbial community structure to process-level kinetics, enabling genus-level calibration of nitrifying bacteria subpopulations

2. Enhancing Parameter Identifiability and Calibration Efficiency

  • Building big data platforms for parameter databases that cover diverse wastewater types (e.g., beer wastewater, printing/dyeing wastewater) to provide recommended parameter sets
  • Applying reaction invariant-based model simplification to reduce the number of ODEs while preserving dynamic accuracy
  • Developing AI-assisted parameter screening tools that leverage Fisher Information Matrix and RDE criteria for automated optimal subset selection

3. Extending ASMs to Emerging Contaminants (ECs)

  • Developing multi-pollutant, multi-pathway, multi-product ASM frameworks for PPCPs and EDCs that are fully integrated with general ASMs
  • Building co-metabolic process-based (CPB) models that account for both AOB and HB contributions to pharmaceutical degradation, as well as sludge adsorption mechanisms
  • Validating comprehensive coupled models at environmentally representative concentrations

4. Combination with Artificial Intelligence

  • Creating online editable parameter databases managed by AI for intelligent parameter recommendation based on influent conditions and effluent targets
  • Improving interpretability of AI models (beyond feature importance rankings) to explain complex microbial metabolic processes
  • Using pattern recognition and auto-generated differential equations to compensate for unknown processes (e.g., PAO-GAO competition, antibiotic transformation)
  • Addressing data quality and representativeness challenges in fluctuating environments with limited datasets

5. Integrated Urban Water System Modeling

  • Overcoming temporal mismatch between sewer, WWTP, and river models to develop truly integrated sewer-plant-river simulation frameworks
  • Incorporating sulfate, sulfides, volatile fatty acids, and methane as independent state variables in ASM matrices with coupled acid-base chemistry and gas-liquid mass transfer
  • Introducing sub-models for competition between sulfate-reducing bacteria and methanogenic archaea

6. Fossil-Derived CO2CO_2CO2‚Äã Emissions

  • Developing standardized methods to quantify direct carbon emissions from fossil-derived carbon in wastewater, as currently no standardized approach exists

Q: Summarize the main points of the paper.

Claude Sonnet 4.6:

This paper is a comprehensive systematic review of the developments, applications, and future perspectives of Activated Sludge Models (ASMs) in wastewater treatment. The main points are as follows:

1. Historical Development of ASMs

  • ASMs originated from the Monod equation-based models of the 1950s and were formalized by IWA in 1983 using the Gujer matrix framework
  • The series progressed from ASM1 (C and N removal, 1987) ‚Üí ASM2/ASM2d (biological and chemical P removal, 1995/1999) ‚Üí ASM3 (intracellular storage-focused, 1999)
  • Commercial software (GPS-X, BioWin, WEST) built upon ASM frameworks has lowered the barrier to engineering application

2. Mechanism Extensions of ASMs

  • For suspended growth processes (MBR, OD, CASS): Extended ASMs incorporating SMP (UAP + BAP) and EPS formation-degradation dynamics significantly improve prediction of membrane fouling and effluent quality; the "anaerobic nucleus" theory was applied to OD simulation
  • For sludge-related challenges: ASM1-based filamentous bulking model integrating kinetic selection, substrate diffusion limitation, and filamentous backbone theories enables quantitative prediction of bulking; ASM2d extended with predation-induced and virus-induced decay quantifies sludge reduction potential (predation ‚â§21%, virus infection ‚â§9%)
  • For phosphorus removal: Models range from ASM2d (10 processes) to META-ASM (81 processes), with TUDP integrating glycogen metabolism; plant-wide models couple ASM with ADM and physicochemical precipitation models
  • For N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO emissions: Multi-pathway models integrating AOB dual pathways (NH2OHNH_2OHNH2‚ÄãOH oxidation + autotrophic denitrification) with heterotrophic denitrification and KLŒ±K_L\alphaKL‚ÄãŒ±-based stripping modules enable prediction of both liquid-phase concentration and gas emission flux
  • For CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã emissions: ASM1 extended with methanotrophic bacteria (XMOBX_{MOB}XMOB‚Äã) and CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã substrate (SCH4S_{CH4}SCH4‚Äã) state variables to simulate CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã oxidation
  • For industrial wastewater: ASM3 adapted for coking wastewater with thiocyanate/cyanide inhibition terms; ASM1 simplified for pulp wastewater; model structure adjusted based on specific treatment objectives

3. Multi-Scale Model Integration

  • ASM + ADM: Two approaches ‚Äî supermodel (fully integrated, element-conserving) and interface method (Copp interface improved by Nopens et al.) ‚Äî enable plant-wide simulation from aerobic biological treatment to anaerobic digestion
  • ASM + CFD: ASMs embedded as biochemical source terms in CFD equations enable 3D simulation of velocity fields, DO distribution, sludge settling, and pollutant degradation in full-scale ODs; SPH provides an alternative mesh-free Lagrangian approach
  • ASM + Hydrodynamic models: Coupling with sewer models and river models (Streeter-Phelps) enables integrated urban water system simulation covering sewer-WWTP-receiving water body continuum
  • ASM + Machine Learning: Three paradigms: (1) ASMs generate training databases for ML; (2) ML (BPNN, Decision Forest, Bayesian optimization) facilitates real-time parameter identification; (3) deep embedding of ASM knowledge into ML reward functions (Q-learning, MARL) for intelligent control and autonomous calibration

4. Future Perspectives

  • Refine model theory: two-step nitrification, reactive SST models, omics data integration
  • Enhance parameter identifiability: Fisher Information Matrix, RDE criterion, big data parameter databases
  • Extend to emerging contaminants: multi-pathway CPB models for PPCPs and EDCs
  • Integrate AI: online editable parameter databases, interpretable AI, auto-generated differential equations for unknown processes

  • ASMs are foundational for wastewater treatment modeling but require extensions to handle modern challenges like MBRs, phosphorus removal, and greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Mechanism extensions have improved accuracy by incorporating SMP/EPS, detailed phosphorus metabolic pathways, and N2ON_2ON2‚ÄãO/CH4CH_4CH4‚Äã emission pathways.
  • Cross-scale coupled simulations (ASMs + ADM, CFD, hydrodynamic models, ML) enable comprehensive plant-wide and system-wide modeling.
  • Future ASM development must balance model complexity with parameter identifiability, leveraging AI and machine learning to optimize calibration and real-time application.
  • Addressing emerging contaminants and resource recovery are critical next steps for ASM evolution.

  • Future Directions: The field needs to move towards integrating micro-scale omics data, modeling emerging contaminants, and using AI to balance accuracy with computational efficiency.
